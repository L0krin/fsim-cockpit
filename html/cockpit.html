Background:

A few years ago I got an Arduino and had a lot of fun playing around with it creating various circuits. After awhile I ended up wanting to make permanent projects that I would keep together so I ended up buying 5 Pro Micro boards on ebay. Then after a couple years I got busy with school and raising my son, so they ended up collecting dust. I'm now in my senior year, and I plan on earning my pilot's license after graduation. I have a flight yoke and rudder pedals, but clicking around the virtual cockpit is getting old. I recently bought a 3D printer, so that really pulled me back into the world of small electronics. So after doing some research and modeling I found some useful sites to help me bring this project to life.

Parts:

I already have the flight yoke and rudder pedals, but I really wanted some more realism. So one day I was browsing Thingiverse for something to print with my printer and I ended up searching "Flight Simulator". The first result was an Attitude Indicator which caught my attention because I initially thought "How would they do such a thing with a 3D printer?" so as I started reading into creating it, I started slipping deeper into the rabbit hole.
Just after that search I've already got the following gauges on my list of things to print:
Attitude Indicator,
3.5" Gauge,
Turn Coordinator,
I still need to find an altitude indicator and compass, but those will keep my printer busy for a little while. In the meantime I ordered some SG90 servos so I can build the gauges when they finish printing. SG90 Servos.

The Project:

At this point I had my project in mind. I wanted to build a flight simulator cockpit, but I know how fast this can turn into a money pit, so I'm calling it a "budget cockpit"...whatever that means. Essentially the idea is that I will build a cockpit as cheap as I can using what I have or can get cheap. Luckily having a 3D printer makes things a lot easier. I'm willing to sacrifice authenticity where necessary in order to cut costs. In the end it will never be like the real thing, but I expect a little more realism can only help my quest for a private pilot's license. So I came up with a basic list of what I want to start with:

  • Keep the cost low, some authenticity will be sacrificed to cut costs.
  • Compatible with X-Plane 11 simulator.
  • Use as much free and open-source software and hardware as possible.
  • Document progress, and provide links and files so that anyone who may want to replicate the cockpit can.
  • If licensing allows, keep a repository of all the files I used so that they can be preserved.
  • Keep a log of whats been done, and what I'm currently working on.
  • Provide a list of future additions.
  • Include Pictures, Videos, and Timelapses of the build process.

The Goal:

The primary goal here is to create an environment where I can practice for flight training. I also like to play with FSEconomy which adds a lot of depth to the flight simulator. Trying to conserve fuel and make as much money as possible makes flying much more fun. I used to spend hours just practicing pattern work and flying around practicing manuvers and then landings. Soon I'll have owned X-Plane 11 for a year, in that time I've logged over 40 hours of flight time, but of those 40 only 1-2 were actually cross-country flights, and I had no idea how to navigate. I've been playing FSEconomy for a week and have already learned a lot and logged several hours of cross-country flight. For anyone who enjoys flight but is bored just flying around the airport, I highly recommend FSEconomy. Oh by the way, FSEconomy is free and open-source.
